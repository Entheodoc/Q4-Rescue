**Purpose**  
This document defines the standardized operational workflow for the JASA-EP Medication Adherence Outreach Program. The workflow is designed to systematically identify, prioritize, and support members at risk of medication nonadherence with the primary purpose of protecting adherence performance by using the Percentage of Days Covered (PDC) as a proxy metric. And ensuring the PDC is at least 80% by the end of the calendar year. This document serves as an operational standard and is intended for direct execution by program staff after review by leadership.

This revamped version of the workflow reflects both the Institute for Healthcare Improvement (IHI) Model for Improvement and the National Committee for Quality Assurance (NCQA) population-health framework. Following the IHI model, the workflow is structured around continuous improvement cycles: planning, implementing, studying outcomes, and refining interventions. In alignment with the NCQA framework, the workflow defines a target population, stratifies members by risk, delivers tailored interventions, measures outcomes, and repeats the improvement cycle. Together they provide a solid foundation for continuous improvement at both the population and individual levels.

In previous iterations, the program demonstrated strong operational performance, achieving adherence improvements above 90% in the proportion of days covered (PDC) across the three CMS Star Rating measures for two consecutive years. However, the program lacked the clinical data necessary to directly evaluate health-outcome improvements. This revised workflow introduces the systematic collection of clinical indicators, including hemoglobin A1C, blood pressure, and lipid panel metrics, to allow the program to evaluate health-outcome improvements by the end of the measurement period.

**Workflow Overview**  
The workflow consists of seven structured phases:

1. Intake  
2. Contact  
3. Assessment  
4. Intervention  
5. Follow-Up  
6. Closure  
7. Monitoring

The cases enter the workflow at the Intake Phase. Subsequent phase progression is conditional and outcome-dependent. Not all cases will pass through every phase. **The Intake, Closure and Monitoring phases are mandatory and are experienced by each case in its lifecycle.** Navigation through all other phases (Contact, Assessment, Intervention and Follow-Up) is dependent on operational findings and risk stratification.

**Phase Definitions and Operational Standards**  
The phases of the workflow have defined objectives, tasks, documentation standards, entry and exit criteria.

Phase movement is controlled by:

* Operational findings and  
* Risk stratification.

Based on strictly defined criteria, cases may:

* Move forward to the next phase,   
* Repeat a previous phase or  
* Repeat the current phase.

Every member referred to the program remains in the workflow until at least the end of the calendar year and at most, until January 31st of the following calendar year. This being independent of historical or current enrollment status. Through its lifecycle each case might be referred as:

* Active.   
  Corresponding to phases 1 through 6 (Intake, Contact, Assessment, Intervention, Follow-Up and Closure). During these phases we actively work towards adherence improvement.

* Inactive.  
  Corresponding to phase 7, Monitoring. During this phase, we monitor medication refill-related events to determine risk of non-adherence for inactive patients and flag those that were previously enrolled for potential re-enrollment.

  

#### **Phase 1 \- Intake**

##### **Objective** Formally initiate and validate a case for a member identified by our partner as being at risk of medication nonadherence.

##### **Phase Overview** The Intake phase establishes the operational and structural foundation upon which each case progresses through the workflow.

During this phase, referral data is formally entered into internal systems, enrollment eligibility is assessed, and profile information is validated. Each case undergoes structured eligibility screening, including urgency review relative to the Goal Not Reachable Date, geographic boundaries, language requirements, and health plan status enrollment.

In addition, member profile information is validated across systems to ensure data consistency, and all available contact information is consolidated to minimize friction during outreach.

The Intake phase functions as a control gate, ensuring that only accurate, eligible, and actionable cases proceed to the Contact phase.

##### **Intake Phase Entry Criteria** The Intake phase begins upon receipt of referral files from the health plan via email. This is the only entry criteria to this phase. Referrals are submitted on a recurring basis, typically weekly, but frequency and volume may vary depending on the client needs and the program’s current operational capacity. When referral volume exceeds operational capacity, referrals may be staged or deferred according to workload management guidelines. Current workload guidelines, as of 03/09/2026, define a maximum of 65 active cases per team member. **Cases on the Monitoring phase are considered Inactive and do not count towards the standard workload.** Cases are considered **Active** in phases Intake through Closure.   **Intake Phase Tasks**  **1-Case data entry.**

The new referral information and claims profiles of the referred cases are retrieved from the email attachments section and added to the Internal Tracking, Monthly Form, and Google Drive. The cases are listed based on zip code proximity to maximize chances for commute consolidation during in-person interventions. All cases must be recorded before proceeding to eligibility screening.

##### **2-Eligibility screening.**

Each case is assessed for enrollment eligibility following a queue organized by proximity to the Goal Not Reachable Date. The screening criteria are:

A)-Urgency Eligibility  
Any case with less than 3 natural weeks from the Goal Not Reachable Date is flagged and reported to the immediate supervisor as the time remaining may be insufficient to influence adherence outcomes. Further workflow progression of cases flagged during this validation step is at the discretion of the supervisor. 

B)-Geographical Eligibility  
Any case with a personal address outside the boundaries of the service area is flagged and reported to the immediate supervisor. Cases flagged as outside the service area are immediately proposed for advancement to the Closure phase.

C)-Language Eligibility  
Any case where the primary language spoken by the member or the designated representative is not English, Spanish, or Mandarin is flagged and escalated to the supervisor. Further workflow progression of the flagged cases will be determined by the supervisor.

D)-Health Plan Status Eligibility  
Any case with statuses as Disenrolled or Expired on the Arine platform is flagged and escalated to the supervisor. Further workflow progression of the flagged cases will be determined by the supervisor.

##### **3-Profile information validation.**

This task ensures consistency across all member records. Inconsistency of records is also a cause of medication nonadherence as it creates considerable friction in the system. Every case is reviewed for validation of the following information across the Arine Platform and the Internal Tracking and any discrepancies must be documented and resolved before proceeding.

A)-Date of birth   
B)-Member First, Middle, and Last Name(s)  
C)-Member Address (Current and Historical)

**4-Contact information consolidation.**  
A detailed search is performed on the Arine and Okta platforms searching for any additional contact numbers. Any findings should be added to the Internal Tracking. Each entry must include a note stating the source platform. 

**Intake Phase Documentation**  
Following the previous tasks, a note stating: “Active with JASA” is added to the member profile notes section of the Arine Platform. The note automatically records the team member name, along with the date and time of the entry. This task concludes the Intake phase and signals progression to the next phase. 

##### **Intake Phase Exit Criteria** The Intake phase is considered complete when all of the following conditions have been met:

A)-The referral file has been received and the case has been entered into the Internal Tracking and the Monthly Form. And the claims profile received have been added to Google Drive.

B)-Enrollment eligibility has been formally screened and dispositioned as:

* Eligible for enrollment, or  
* Proposed for closure, or   
* Escalated to the supervisor and received written instruction to proceed to the next phase.

C)-Urgency review has been completed, including validation of proximity to the Goal Not Reachable Date.

D)-Geographic, language, and health plan enrollment status eligibility checks have been completed.

E)-Member profile information has been validated for consistency across Internal Tracking and Arine Platform records.

F)-All available contact information has been consolidated and documented.

G)-An “Active with JASA” note has been added to the Arine Platform. 

Upon completion of these criteria, the case may proceed to:

* Contact Phase (if eligible for enrollment), or  
* Closure Phase (if ineligible or supervisor-directed).

##### **Intake Phase Timeline**  The Intake phase must be completed within five business days of referral receipt, provided operational capacity is available. If referral volume exceeds capacity limits, the direct supervisor must be informed. Flagged cases requiring supervisory review must be escalated via email on the same business day they are identified and a response should be received in the next 5 natural days from escalation.

####       **Phase 2 \- Contact**

##### **Objective** Establish direct communication with the member in order to initiate engagement, obtain consent to enroll in the program, and provide preliminary, urgent intervention if possible.  **Phase Overview** The Contact phase is the first direct member-facing phase of the workflow.

During this phase, structured outreach attempts are conducted to reach the member, assess immediate nonadherence risk, provide urgent support and offer enrollment.

The Contact phase functions as an engagement gate. It determines whether the case progresses to the Assessment phase, or moves toward Closure due to unreachability, circumstantial ineligibility (hospitalizations, long-term travel) or explicit refusal to receive services.

##### **Contact Phase Entry Criteria** The Contact phase begins once the tasks of the Intake phase have been completed and the case has been deemed eligible for enrollment. Cases flagged as ineligible during Intake may proceed to Contact only after supervisory review and approval.

##### **Contact Phase Tasks**

##### **1-Case assignment.**

The supervisor assigns the case to a specific  team member according to the member’s preferred language, geographic area of residence, and the current team’s workload.

**2-Consecutive Outreach Attempts**  
Following the service level agreements the designated teammate attempts to contact the members via phone call on five different days. A contact attempt is considered an attempt to reach the member through each of the contact numbers available. As an example, if a member has three phone contact numbers available, a call to each of those contacts is considered one attempt.

To ensure maximum chances of a successful contact, calls should be placed on different days of the week and at varying times, including weekends and early evenings. Each call attempt should consist of **two consecutive dial attempts**. If offered a voicemail entry on the first dial, it should be discarded. If offered a voicemail entry on the second dial, the team member should proceed leaving a clear voice message explaining the reason for the call along with the team member name and a call back number. 

After each completed contact attempt, the outcome, successful or unsuccessful, must be recorded on the internal tracking, including the date and time of the attempt. Additionally, the first time an attempt is successful, the date, time and contact number used must be recorded on a dedicated area of the tracker.

A contact attempt is considered successful if the member or an authorized representative is reached, regardless of the outcome of the enrollment offer. Voicemail access **does not** count as a successful attempt.

A contact attempt is considered unsuccessful if neither the member nor an authorized representative is reached. Voicemail access **does** count as an unsuccessful attempt.

Depending on the outcome of each attempt and the contact attempt number (\#1 through \#5), the case might proceed to the next workflow phase. All possible outcomes are outlined below along with their respective next action and phase transition.

**Contact Attempt Successful:**

A)-Accepts enrollment. → Perform Preliminary Phone-based Intervention and/or Schedule next In-Person Intervention or Phone-based Intervention. →  Phase 3 Assessment.

B)-Declines enrollment. → Record service refusal. → Phase 6 Closure.

C)-Defers enrollment for **more** than 2 weeks.  →  Record service refusal. → Phase 6 Closure.

D)-Defers enrollment for **less** than 2 weeks. → Schedule next attempt.→ Stay in Phase 2\.

E)-Member has expired. → Confirm and record expiration. → Phase 6 Closure.

F)-Member or representative presents a language barrier. → Escalate. → Stay in Phase 2\.

G)-Out of the service area for **more** than 2 weeks. → Record ineligibility. → Phase 6 Closure.

H)-Out of the service area for **less** than 2 weeks. →Schedule the next attempt.→Stay in Phase 2\.

I)-Member hospitalized or in a long term care facility. → Record ineligibility → Phase 6 Closure.

**Contact Attempt Unsuccessful with at least one valid line:**  
A)-Contact attempt \#1 → Record attempt. → Schedule attempt \#2.

B)-Contact attempt \#2 → Reach the pharmacy for additional contact. → Record attempt → Schedule attempt \#3.

C)-Contact attempt \#3  → Record attempt. →  Schedule attempt \#4.

D)-Contact attempt \#4  → Record attempt. →  Schedule attempt \#5.

E)-Contact attempt \#5  → Record attempt. →  Phase 6 Closure. (Unable to Reach.)

   
**Contact Attempt Unsuccessful with all available contacts as invalid line(s):**  
A)-Contact attempt \#1 →  Reach the pharmacy for additional contact. 

If a new alternative contact number obtained → Record attempt → Schedule Contact attempt \#2

If no alternative contact number obtained → Record attempt → Phase 6 Closure.

**Contact Phase Documentation**  
The team member case assignment is documented on the Internal Tracking Document.  
The outcome of each of the potential 5 contact attempts must be recorded. 

If the attempt is **successful** the notes of the attempt will contain one or several of the following:

A)-Name and/or role of the person reached.  
B)-Number used to reach.  
C)-Outcome of the enrollment offer, be it accepted, refused or deferred.  
D)-If a Phone Intervention was performed.  
E)-Next action: \-Intervention scheduled. or \-Next attempt scheduled.  
F)-Transition to Phase 6 Closure and its cause. (Refusal or specific ineligibility qualifier).

If the attempt is **unsuccessful** with at least one valid line, the notes of the attempt will contain one or several of the following:

A)-Next attempt scheduled.  
B)-Accessibility to voicemail: \-Voicemail full.  \-Left voice message. \-No voicemail available.  
C)-Pharmacy Outreach for additional contacts and its outcome. (During contact attempt \#2 only)  
D)-Transition to Phase 6 Closure. (Unable to reach after contact attempt \#5 only)

If the attempt is **unsuccessful** with all available contacts as invalid line(s), the notes of the attempt will contain one or several of the following:

A)-Pharmacy Outreach for additional contacts and its outcome.  
B)-All contact numbers attempted along with invalid line reason:-Wrong number. \-Disconnected.  
C)-Transition to Phase 6 Closure and its cause. (Wrong number and/or disconnected).

##### **Contact Phase Exit Criteria** The Contact phase is considered complete when one of the following conditions has been met:  A)-Any contact attempt has been successful or enrollment deferral is more than 2 weeks.

B)-Five unsuccessful contact attempts have been completed using **at least one valid line**.  
C)-A contact attempt has been unsuccessful and all available contacts are **invalid line(s).**

##### **Contact Phase Timeline**  The Contact phase must be completed within a natural week from Intake phase completion, provided operational capacity is available. If referral volume exceeds capacity limits, or edge cases appear during the phase, the direct supervisor must be informed.

##### **Phase 3 \- Assessment**

##### **Objective** Gather additional information, evaluate the current status of each case, and strategize the intervention to protect or restore PDC performance at or above 80%. 

**Phase Overview**  
The Assessment phase consists of two broad stages:

**1-**Data gathering through pharmacy data review, and  
**2-**Structured evaluation including Reference Measure selection, risk stratification and Case Matrix positioning.

During the first stage, additional prescription information is gathered through direct communication with the member’s dispensing pharmacy or pharmacies.

During the second stage, the collected information is evaluated to determine the **Reference Medication, Prescription Status, and Adherence Risk Status** of each case. These classifications are based on the medication’s next fill date, current active refill availability, projected active refill availability, and current dispensing state.

Because these classifications interact in multiple ways, they are treated as categorical dimensions whose possible combinations are enumerated in a **Case Matrix.** Predefined compatibility rules are then used to distinguish valid operational states from logically inconsistent combinations.

This structured approach allows complex prescription data to be translated into standardized intervention pathways that guide the actions taken during the subsequent Intervention phase.

##### **Assessment Phase Entry Criteria** The Assessment phase begins once the member has been successfully enrolled in the program.

##### **Assessment Phase Tasks**

##### **1-Pharmacy information gathering.** A comprehensive prescription review is conducted by contacting the member’s dispensing pharmacy(s).

During this task, the team member verifies and updates the following information for each qualifying medication:

* Most recent fill date.  
* Days supply filled on the most recent fill date.

Additionally, the team member gathers the following information, that is **not provided by our partner**, for each qualifying medication:

* Pickup date.  
* Remaining refills and their corresponding days supply.  
* Any currently filled prescriptions pending pickup, along with a timeline before return to stock and claim reversal.  
* Any prescriptions recently returned to stock.  
* Medication delivery service availability.  
* Availability of medication synchronization programs (refill synchronization or blister packaging).

This task ensures that the program operates on real-time dispensing data rather than referral assumptions. It allows accurate calculation of refill gaps and prevents reliance on outdated claims information. This task establishes the factual medication timeline upon which the **Reference Medication**, the **Prescription Status** and the **Adherence Risk Status** are determined.

**2-Determination of the Reference Medication**  
The reference medication is any medication that qualifies for one of the monitored measures as recommended by the Pharmacy Quality Alliance, approved by the Centers for Medicare & Medicaid Services, and detailed on the accompanying attachments of the [Medicare 2026 Part C & D Star Ratings Technical Notes](https://www.cms.gov/files/document/2026-star-ratings-technical-notes.pdf). When multiple medications qualify for the same measure, the **Reference Medication is defined as the qualifying medication whose total available coverage extends furthest into the future.**

Total available coverage is determined by adding:

* The next fill date of the medication and,  
* The total days supply associated with all prescription refills that will remain active at the upcoming projected fill events up to the end of the calendar year.

The medication whose combined coverage period extends furthest into the future is selected as the **Reference Medication**.

A refill is considered active when it is less than 365 days past from its prescription date. A projected active refill is a refill that will be less than 365 days from its prescription date on the next fill date(s) up to the end of the calendar year.

**3-Determination of Prescription Status.**  
Using updated pharmacy data and referral information, the team member determines each measure’s qualifying medication(s) current **Prescription Status** using four distinctive, mutable, but mutually exclusive categories.

**A)-Prescription Missing.**   
There is **no active prescription** on file at any pharmacy for the qualifying medication(s) and the next fill date precedes the current date.

**B)-Prescription Unfilled.**   
An active prescription exists, but **no paid claim has been placed for the current fill cycle**.

**C)-Prescription Filled.**   
A claim has been placed, the medication is currently at the pharmacy(s) awaiting pickup, awaiting delivery, or under custody of a private courier. 

**D)-Prescription Dispensed.**   
The medication has left the pharmacy and is either with the member or on its way to the member via mail. Note that private courier delivery arranged by the dispensing pharmacy does not count as medication dispensed since the medication might still be returned to the pharmacy and the claim reversed.

##### **4-Determination of Adherence Risk Status.**

Using updated pharmacy data and referral information, the team member determines each measure’s risk status. **For the purposes of our program, Adherence Risk Status is a program-defined, refill- and dispensing-based classification used to represent the current and near-term risk that a case will fail to maintain adherence performance under 80% PDC.** The construct is organized into five distinctive, mutable, and mutually exclusive categories.

**A)**\-**Non-Adherent**: NFD precedes current date.

**B)-Adherent – Critical Risk**: NFD succeeds current date, but medication is currently filled and awaiting pickup/delivery. (The claim will be reversed unless the medication is picked up in less than 10 days including weekends and federal holidays..

**C)-Adherent – High Risk**: NFD is \<30 days away, no active refill, not currently awaiting pickup/delivery.

**D)-Adherent – Medium Risk**: NFD is 30–59 days away, no active refill, not currently awaiting pickup/delivery.

**E)-Adherent – Low Risk**: NFD is ≥60 days away and not currently awaiting pickup/delivery, **or** NFD is 30–59 days away, at least one active refill is available, and not currently awaiting pickup/delivery.

**5-Case Matrix positioning.**  
Following the determination of the **Adherence Risk Status** and the **Prescription Status**, the categories are placed in the **Case Matrix** with the goal of decreasing complexity and clarifying the most appropriate intervention plan. 

The current **Case Matrix** is composed of 2 main axes that are determined on the previous tasks of the Assessment phase:

\-**Prescription Status**  
\-**Adherence Risk Status**

Each of these categories has pre-defined statuses:

**Prescription Status:**  
A)-Prescription Missing  
B)-Prescription Unfilled  
C)-Prescription Filled  
D)-Prescription Dispensed

**Adherence Risk Status:**  
1)-Non-Adherent  
2)-Adherent \- Low Risk  
3)-Adherent \- Medium Risk  
4)-Adherent \- High Risk  
5)-Adherent \- Critical Risk

Combining both categories yields 20 theoretical case combinations. However, 14 of the combinations are mutually exclusive and do not occur in practice. Foreseeing potential future automation needs, Appendix A contains an expanded list of the **Case Matrix** including all case possibilities regardless of being real or not. Note that the following **Case Matrix** is rather rudimentary and designed for human-level inference. With the addition of digital tooling, the number of axes can be increased to accommodate further personalization of interventions.

As a matrix filter, the **Prescription Status** and **Adherence Risk Status** must remain logically compatible. If a combination violates the definitions of either category, the case must be reclassified before proceeding with intervention planning. Below only the 6 combinations classified as real are explained and followed by a summarized intervention plan. 

**Case Matrix Expansion \- Real Combinations**

### **(A1) Prescription Missing \+ Non-Adherent** 

**Validated state:** No active prescription exists for the reference medication, and the measure is overdue (the next fill date has passed).

### **(B1) Prescription Unfilled \+ Non-Adherent** 

**Validated state:** Active prescription exists, but the reference medication has not been filled, and the measure is overdue (the next fill date has passed).

### **(C5) Prescription Filled \+ Adherent – Critical Risk**

**Validated state:** Medication is currently at the pharmacy (or with private courier) waiting to be dispensed; the reference medication is currently in the Critical-risk pickup/delivery window.

### **(D2) Prescription Dispensed \+ Adherent – Low Risk** 

**Validated state:** The reference medication has been dispensed; runway ≥60 days; refill available/active on next fill date; not awaiting pickup/delivery.

**(D3) Prescription Dispensed \+ Adherent – Medium Risk** 

**Validated state:** The reference medication has been dispensed; runway 30–59 days; refill not available/active on next fill date; not awaiting pickup/delivery.

### **(D4) Prescription Dispensed \+ Adherent – High Risk** 

**Validated state:** The reference medication has been dispensed; runway \<30 days; refill not available/active on next fill date; not awaiting pickup/delivery.

**6-Determination of the Reference Measure.**  
After positioning each measure on the Case Matrix, the measure with the highest urgency should be used as the **Reference Measure** to describe the current status of the case. The **Reference Measure** is defined as **the highest urgency measure whose total available coverage is the lowest.** The urgency rule is organized on the list below from highest to lowest urgency level:

1-(A1) Prescription Missing \+ Non-Adherent  
The case is **already overdue** and there is **no active prescription**, the medication cannot be filled without prescriber action. This is the highest risk group and the most time-sensitive.

2-(B1) Prescription Unfilled \+ Non-Adherent  
The case is **already overdue**, but at least an active prescription exists. The quickest direction change of the PDC trend is possible if the pharmacy has the medication in stock and is willing to fill immediately.

3-(C5) Prescription Filled \+ Adherent – Critical Risk  
The medication is sitting in a **“failure window”** (awaiting pickup/delivery/private courier) where it can revert (return-to-stock / reversal risk). This block presents risk for imminent negative direction change of the PDC trend even if the next fill date hasn’t passed yet.

4-(D4) Prescription Dispensed \+ Adherent – High Risk  
**Medication has been dispensed** and the patient is currently covered, **but runway is \<30 days and there is no refill available/active.** This will imminently change the direction of the PDC trend unless a new prescription is obtained quickly.

5-(D3) Prescription Dispensed \+ Adherent – Medium Risk  
Runway is **30–59 days** with **no refill available/active**. While there is more time available, it still requires a qualifying prescription event before the next fill date.

6-(D2) Prescription Dispensed \+ Adherent – Low Risk  
Runway is **≥60 days**, refill is **available/active**, and there is no medication awaiting pickup/delivery at the pharmacy. This signals ample time for intervention and relative deprioritization. 

When more than one measure for the same case is placed on the same matrix position, it is implied that they have the same urgency level. However, the measure with the lowest available coverage should be selected in this case. Though rare in our program, there might be cases in which measures behave as mathematical twins, given that the quantity of the medication prescribed, refills, dates of fill, and pickup are identical. In this instance, the Reference Measure should be selected following the program priority rule: 

1-DM measure.  
2-RASA measure.  
3-Statin measure. 

**7-Identification of explicit barriers.**  
Following positioning of the case in the Case Matrix, the team member must identify any **explicit barriers** contributing to current or imminent nonadherence.

An explicit barrier is an observable, verifiable factor that directly interferes with the member’s ability to obtain, take, or continue qualifying medication(s) as prescribed.

Documentation rule: Only barriers supported by evidence from partner-provided data and/or pharmacy-gathered data may be recorded during this task. **Speculation and inferred causes must not be logged as explicit barriers.**

During the Assessment phase of the workflow, the barriers are recorded on the Monthly Form for reporting. While barriers will likely carry different predictive weights for nonadherence risk, the **current dataset size is not sufficient to infer stable weights**, so barrier logging is descriptive and structured, **not yet predictive.**

The potential explicit barriers are as follows:

**A)-High copay.**   
Evidence of a qualifying medication copay above the program-defined affordability threshold.

**B)-Provider services fragmentation.**   
\-Multiple prescribers across the medications qualifying for any measure and/or multiple providers as prescribers of medications qualifying for the same measure in the last six months. 

\-Any of the qualifying medications has been prescribed by a secondary care provider. (e.g., specialist)

**C)-Pharmacy services fragmentation.**   
\-Evidence of more than one pharmacy where claims have been placed in the last 180 days.

**D)-Recent treatment changes.**  
\-Evidence of first time filling or dosage change for a qualifying medication in the last 60 days.

**E)-Polypharmacy.**  
\-Concurrent use of 5 or more medications. 

**F)-Geographic barrier.**  
\-Member residence located more than one mile from the pharmacy(s).

**G)-Synchronization Misalignment.**  
\-Qualifying medications have differing fill dates with more than 7 days in between.

**H)-Language Barrier.**  
\-As stated on the data source, the primary language of the member is different from English.

**8-Preliminary intervention pathway selection**  
Following the determination of the Case Matrix position for each case, a preliminary intervention pathway is selected for execution during the Intervention phase.

**1-(A1) Prescription Missing \+ Non-Adherent**   
→ Contact Prescriber for renewal/new Rx request (urgent). 

If Rx is promised → Schedule Pharmacy Outreach according to the promised timeline → Perform Scheduled Pharmacy Outreach to confirm Rx reception. 

If Rx is denied → Resolve cause of denial. → Contact Prescriber for renewal/new Rx request. → Schedule Pharmacy Outreach according to the promised timeline. → Perform Scheduled Pharmacy Outreach to confirm Rx reception. 

If Rx has not been received, restart the loop with → Contact Prescriber for renewal/new Rx request.

If Rx has been received → Route to (B\*) or (C5) as appropriate.

### **2-(B1) Prescription Unfilled \+ Non-Adherent**  → Immediate Pharmacy Outreach to request fill.  If the fill is promised → Contact the Member to advise pickup according to the promised fill timeline and Schedule Pharmacy Outreach 72-96 hours in the future to confirm pickup. → Perform Scheduled Claims Check.

If the fill is not promised → Resolve cause or transfer prescription. → Contact the Member to advise pickup according to the promised fill timeline and Schedule Pharmacy Outreach 72-96 hours in the future. → Perform Scheduled Claims Check.

If the scheduled claims check result is negative, restart loop with → Immediate Pharmacy Outreach to request fill.

If the scheduled claims check result is positive → Contact Pharmacy and route to (C5) or (D\*) statuses accordingly.

### **3-(C5) Prescription Filled \+ Adherent – Critical Risk** → Contact the Member and advise to pick up.  →Schedule Pharmacy Outreach according to the promised pickup timeline → Perform Scheduled Pharmacy Outreach to confirm Rx has been dispensed. 

→ If Rx has not been dispensed, restart the loop with → Contact the Member and advise to pickup. (Note that after the second member contact advising to pickup, next contacts will offer one-time copay support and delivery).

→ If Rx has been dispensed. → Route to (D\*) statuses accordingly.

### **4-(D4) Prescription Dispensed \+ Adherent – High Risk**  → Contact Prescriber to request Rx  → If Rx is promised → Schedule Pharmacy Outreach according to the promised timeline → Perform Scheduled Pharmacy Outreach to confirm Rx reception. 

If Rx is denied → Resolve cause of denial. → Contact Prescriber for renewal/new Rx request. → Schedule Pharmacy Outreach according to the promised timeline. → Perform Scheduled Pharmacy Outreach to confirm Rx reception. 

If Rx is not received, restart the loop with → Contact Prescriber for renewal/new Rx request.

If Rx is received → Route to (D2) status. 

**5-(D3) Prescription Dispensed \+ Adherent – Medium Risk**   
→ Schedule prescriber contact 30 days before NFD to request Rx → If Rx is promised → Schedule Pharmacy Outreach according to the promised timeline → Perform Scheduled Pharmacy Outreach to confirm Rx reception. 

If Rx is denied → Resolve cause of denial. → Contact Prescriber for renewal/new Rx request. → Schedule Pharmacy Outreach according to the promised timeline. → Perform Scheduled Pharmacy Outreach to confirm Rx reception. 

If Rx is not received, restart the loop with → Contact Prescriber for renewal/new Rx request.

If Rx is received → Route to (D2) status. 

**6-(D2) Prescription Dispensed \+ Adherent – Low Risk**   
→ Scheduled next monthly intervention to provide further education and Schedule Pharmacy Outreach 3 days in advance on the next fill date. → Consider transition to Phase 6 Closure.

**Assessment Phase Documentation**  
The Reference Medication, Reference Measure, Adherence Risk Status, Prescription Status and the Case Matrix position, should be added to the Assessment Results column of the Internal Tracking document.

##### **Assessment Phase Exit Criteria** The Assessment phase is considered complete when all of the following conditions have been met:  A)-Pharmacy data review has been completed for all qualifying medications, including all required dispensing and refill information needed for classification.

##### B)-A Reference Medication has been determined  for each monitored measure.

##### C)-Prescription Status and Adherence Risk Status have been determined for each monitored measure using the most current available data.  D)-A Reference Measure has been assigned for the case using the program’s urgency hierarchy and tie-breaking rules.

##### E)-Each case has been positioned in the Case Matrix, and any logically incompatible classification has been corrected before proceeding.

##### F)-The preliminary intervention pathway has been selected based on the validated Case Matrix position of the Reference Measure.

##### G)-Explicit barriers have been identified and logged using only evidence supported by partner-provided and/or pharmacy-gathered data.

##### H)-All required documentation has been completed on the Monthly Form and the Internal Tracking.

##### **Assessment Phase Timeline** The Assessment phase must be completed within one business day of Contact phase completion (successful enrollment), provided operational capacity and proper pharmacy responsiveness.

##### Cases must be processed in order of Reference Measure urgency, using the Case Matrix hierarchy: (A1) → (B1) → (C5) → (D4) → (D3) → (D2).

##### If pharmacy data review requires callbacks or the pharmacy response is delayed, the case may extend up to two business days, but the team member must document the delay and schedule a defined follow-up time.

##### Any case classified as (A1), (B1), or (C5) must be escalated for same-day execution readiness and completed within the same business day whenever possible.  If operational capacity is exceeded, the direct supervisor must be informed, and cases must be staged strictly by urgency based on the Reference Measure position within the Case Matrix.

##### **Phase 4 \- Intervention**

##### **Objective** Execute the intervention plan required to remove, reduce, or manage the immediate and underlying factors contributing to current or imminent medication nonadherence. This phase aims to protect or restore the case’s PDC trend, ensure at least 80% PDC by the end of the calendar year, strengthen the conditions required for sustained adherence, and generate the information needed to evaluate the effect of the intervention on health outcomes by the end of the case lifecycle.

##### **Phase Overview**

##### The Intervention phase is the execution phase of the workflow.

##### During this phase, the intervention plan selected during the Assessment phase is actively carried out through two structured stages:

##### **Stage 1 – Home Visit Intervention**, and **Stage 2 – Targeted Adherence Coordination**.

##### The Intervention phase functions as the action engine of the workflow. While the Assessment phase classifies the case, identifies explicit barriers, and selects a preliminary pathway, the Intervention phase carries out the real-world actions required to improve adherence performance and stabilize the case.

##### The first stage, the **Home Visit Intervention**, is the central in-person component of the workflow. During this stage, the team evaluates the member in context, provides adherence-focused education, identifies and addresses implicit barriers, reviews the medication environment in the home, creates rapport with the member, and obtains authorization to help the program track information relevant to intervention outcomes.

##### The second stage, **Targeted Adherence Coordination**, consists of the structured logistical and operational work carried out with the pharmacy, provider, member, and when applicable the authorized representative. During this stage, the team member executes the predefined pathway associated with the cases’s validated Case Matrix position, while continuously reassessing whether the pathway should be maintained or revised based on operational findings and updated risk.

##### The Intervention phase is dynamic and may require repeated cycles of in-person intervention, outreach, coordination, confirmation, and reclassification. Cases may remain in this phase until the immediate adherence threat has been reduced, the case has stabilized sufficiently to move to Follow-Up, or the case reaches a condition requiring Closure.

##### 

##### **Intervention Phase Entry Criteria**

##### The Intervention phase begins once all tasks of the Assessment phase have been completed and an intervention plan has been selected for the Reference Measure.

##### To enter the Intervention phase, all of the following conditions must be met:

##### A)-The member has been successfully enrolled in the program.

##### B)-Pharmacy reconciliation has been completed for all qualifying medications relevant to the current intervention strategy.

##### C)-The Reference Medication and Reference Measure have been determined.

##### D)-The Prescription Status, Adherence Risk Status, and Case Matrix position of the Reference Measure have been validated.

##### E)-Explicit barriers supported by partner-provided and/or pharmacy-gathered data have been identified and documented.

##### F)-A preliminary intervention pathway has been selected based on the validated Case Matrix position of the Reference Measure.

##### G)-The case is ready for scheduling or execution of the home visit intervention.

##### Cases classified as (A1), (B1), or (C5) must be prioritized for immediate entry into the Intervention phase due to the presence of current or imminent adherence failure risk.

#####  **Intervention Phase Tasks**

#### **Stage 1 – Home Visit Intervention**

#### **1-Performance of the home visit intervention**

##### The home visit intervention is performed as the first stage of this phase and is a central component of the program’s intervention model.

##### The purpose of the home visit is to evaluate the member in context, provide structured adherence support, identify and address implicit barriers to adherence, improve the member’s understanding of the monitored medications and associated conditions, and establish the interpersonal rapport required to support continuity of communication for the remainder of the case lifecycle.

##### During the home visit, the team member performs the following as applicable:

##### A)-Provides patient education related to all medications currently being monitored under the program, including medications qualifying for the diabetes, RASA, and statin adherence measures, and the associated chronic conditions, including type 2 diabetes mellitus, arterial hypertension, and dyslipidemia.

##### B)-Provides printed educational materials related to the monitored medications and their associated conditions.

##### C)-Provides an updated printed medication list containing all medications the member is currently taking.

##### D)-Provides a pillbox and teaches the member how to use it to organize medications appropriately.

##### E)-Provides a blood pressure monitor, when appropriate, and teaches the member how to use it.

##### F)-Reviews the medications physically present in the home to identify over-accumulation, duplication, expired medications, or disorganized medication storage that may contribute to nonadherence.

##### G)-Teaches the member how to discard medications properly when over-accumulation or expired medications are identified.

##### H)-Screens for implicit barriers and attempts to address them within program scope.

##### I)-Screens for selected social and practical factors that may interfere with adherence.

##### J)-Builds rapport with the member to strengthen trust and support future communication and responsiveness.

##### K)-Obtains authorization, as appropriate, to help the program access and track information relevant to evaluating intervention outcomes over the life of the case.

#### **2-Patient education and self-management support**

##### A structured educational intervention is delivered during the home visit.

##### This task is intended to improve the member’s understanding of why the monitored medications are important, how they relate to the associated chronic conditions, what operational step is currently needed to maintain adherence, and how to organize medication-taking behavior more effectively.

##### Education during the home visit includes, as applicable:

##### A)-Explanation of the purpose of the monitored medications.

##### B)-Explanation of the associated chronic conditions being targeted by the program.

##### C)-Explanation of the importance of timely refill, pickup, renewal, and consistent medication use in preserving adherence.

##### D)-Review of the updated printed medication list.

##### E)-Provision of printed educational materials.

##### F)-Instruction on the use of the pillbox for medication organization.

##### G)-Instruction on blood pressure self-monitoring when a blood pressure device is provided.

##### Education should be delivered using the **teach-back method** as the standard feedback method. The team member should ask the member to explain back, in their own words, how the medications are to be taken and what the next required adherence-related action is. This confirms comprehension and allows misunderstanding to be corrected in real time. When cognitive decline or mental health conditions impede effective communication, education should be provided to the authorized representative. 

#### **3-Blood pressure monitor training and self-monitoring instruction**

##### When the case involves hypertension-related monitoring or when otherwise operationally appropriate, the team member provides a blood pressure monitor and instructs the member on its use.

##### The team member does **not** take the member’s blood pressure during the visit. Instead, the device is demonstrated by using it on the team member themself, both as an instructional method and as a practical demonstration that the device is functioning properly.

##### After the demonstration, the member is instructed to perform blood pressure self-monitoring **twice daily, approximately every 12 hours, for 10 days and record such results on a provided blood pressure log**. Example schedules include 6:00 a.m. and 6:00 p.m., or 8:00 a.m. and 8:00 p.m.

##### This assessment should be introduced near the beginning of the case lifecycle and repeated again before case closure, so the program can begin estimating whether improvements in adherence may have been accompanied by changes in health indicators over the course of the intervention period.

#### **4-Identification and management of implicit barriers**

##### During the home visit, the team member identifies and attempts to address **implicit barriers** to adherence.

##### Implicit barriers are adherence obstacles that may not be directly observable through claims data, referral data, or pharmacy data review alone, but that become apparent through in-person interaction, environmental observation, structured questioning, and member behavior.

##### These may include, but are not limited to:

##### A)-Low health literacy or difficulty understanding medication instructions.

##### B)-Language barriers affecting the member’s communication with the primary care provider or prescribing clinician.

##### C)-Weak rapport with the PCP and/or prescribing clinician, creating friction that may reduce medication follow-through.

##### D)-Cognitive decline or cognitive difficulty interfering with medication management.

##### E)-Mental health issues that appear to be interfering with adherence behavior.

##### F)-Cultural beliefs or concerns affecting medication acceptance or consistent use.

##### G)-Medication over-accumulation suggesting confusion, nonuse, or poor regimen organization.

##### H)-Pharmacy fragmentation that may be interfering with refill continuity.

##### I)-Social and environmental factors that appear to interfere with adherence.

##### When identified, these barriers should be addressed to the extent that they fall within program scope. Findings must be documented carefully and descriptively, avoiding unsupported diagnostic conclusions.

#### **5-Screening for social determinants of health and related functional barriers**

##### During the home visit, the team member screens for social and practical barriers that may be associated with nonadherence.

##### These include, as applicable:

##### A)-Transportation barriers.

##### B)-Food-access concerns relevant to medication-taking routines or chronic disease self-management.

##### C)-Loneliness or social isolation when these appear to interfere with continuity of care or medication routines.

##### D)-Fall-prevention concerns or mobility limitations that may affect the member’s ability to retrieve, organize, or take medications safely.

##### E)-Other observable social or environmental conditions that may be affecting adherence behavior.

##### The purpose of this screening is not to convert the intervention into a full social-services assessment, but to identify modifiable factors relevant to adherence and act on them when operationally feasible.

#### **6-Pharmacy consolidation and medication-environment optimization**

##### During the home visit, the team member assesses whether pharmacy fragmentation and medication disorganization are contributing to nonadherence.

##### When appropriate, the team member attempts to reduce this friction by:

##### A)-Encouraging consolidation of prescriptions into a single pharmacy when feasible.

##### B)-Discussing referral to a mail-service or online pharmacy when this is operationally appropriate and likely to improve continuity.

##### C)-Reviewing how medications are stored and organized in the home.

##### D)-Addressing medication over-accumulation and teaching proper discard practices.

##### E)-Recommending the use of the pillbox to support day-to-day organization. 

#### **7-Rapport development and outcome-tracking authorization**

##### A central objective of the home visit is to create rapport with the member.

##### This rapport-building function is operationally important because it increases the likelihood that the member will continue responding to outreach, disclose relevant barriers, follow through with next steps, and remain engaged with the program throughout the remainder of the calendar-year case lifecycle.

##### During this stage, the team member also seeks authorization, as appropriate, to support access to information needed to understand the outcomes of the intervention, including clinical indicators or externally held information relevant to evaluating changes over time.  It is important to note that, although some teammates may have extensive clinical training, within the scope of this program they function operationally as Community Health Workers. As such, their intervention is strictly limited to adherence-related education and support and does not include taking vital signs, advising medication discontinuation, recommending alternative therapies, or proposing any other modification to a licensed clinician’s prescribed regimen.

##### **Intervention Phase Documentation**

##### All Intervention phase activity must be documented in the Internal Tracking columns named Intervention 1 through 5 and, for the case of implicit barriers, on the Monthly Form.  Documentation for this phase must include the following, as applicable:

##### A)-Date and time of each intervention action performed.

##### B)-Type of intervention performed: \-Prescriber outreach. \-Pharmacy outreach. \-Member outreach. \-Barrier-resolution support. \-Scheduled confirmation outreach.

##### C)-Name and role of the person reached, when contact is successful.

##### D)-Medication and measure relevant to the intervention, including whether the action was performed for the Reference Medication.

##### E)-Outcome of each intervention step, including: \-Prescription requested. \-Prescription promised. \-Prescription denied. \-Prescription received by the pharmacy. \-Medication filled. \-Medication dispensed. \-Pickup advised. \-Delivery coordinated. \-Copay support offered or provided. \-Change the 90-day supply. \-Barrier unresolved. \-Barrier resolved.

##### F)-Any promised next step and its expected timeline.

##### G)-Any confirmed change in Prescription Status, Adherence Risk Status, or Case Matrix position.

##### H)-The resulting next action and next scheduled follow-up date, if applicable.

##### I)-Transition to the next phase and the reason for that transition.

##### When an intervention requires repeated outreach, each cycle must be documented as a distinct action sequence. When no operational change has yet occurred, the record must clearly state that the case remains pending confirmation.

##### **Intervention Phase Exit Criteria**

##### The Intervention phase is considered complete when one of the following conditions has been met:

##### A)-The active intervention pathway has been executed and the immediate adherence threat has been reduced or resolved, allowing transition to Phase 5 Follow-Up.

##### B)-The case has been reclassified into a stable lower-risk operational state and no immediate additional intervention action is required at that time.

##### C)-The intervention pathway has reached a terminal outcome requiring Phase 6 Closure, including but not limited to: \-Member refusal of continued services. \-Member expiration. \-Confirmed long-term ineligibility. \-Supervisor-directed closure.

##### D)-The case remains active but the current intervention cycle is complete and the next required action is time-dependent, scheduled, and more appropriately managed under Follow-Up.

##### A case must not exit the Intervention phase while a same-day or near-term high-risk action remains pending without a clearly assigned next step.

##### **Intervention Phase Timeline**

##### The Intervention phase must begin on the same business day that the Assessment phase is completed whenever the Reference Measure is classified as (A1), (B1), or (C5). These cases represent the highest operational urgency and must be acted upon immediately whenever operational capacity allows.

##### For cases classified as (D4), the initial intervention action should begin within one business day of Assessment completion.

##### For cases classified as (D3), the intervention plan should be scheduled according to the projected refill timeline, but the first required prescriber-facing action should be initiated no later than 30 days before the next fill date.

##### For cases classified as (D2), intervention may consist of lower-intensity reinforcement, education, or preemptive scheduling and may transition more rapidly toward Follow-Up when no immediate adherence threat is perceived.

##### Once the phase has begun, all promised callbacks, confirmation contacts, and scheduled pharmacy or prescriber checks must be completed within the timeline committed during the prior intervention step. Any delay, failed callback, or missed response window must be documented and followed by a rescheduled action.

##### If operational capacity is exceeded, cases must be prioritized according to the validated urgency hierarchy of the Reference Measure: (A1) → (B1) → (C5) → (D4) → (D3) → (D2).

##### **Phase 5 \- Follow-Up** 

**Objective**  
Reassess the case after active intervention, confirm whether the effects of the intervention are being sustained over time, identify any new or persistent barriers affecting adherence, and determine whether the case should remain stable, return to active intervention, or proceed toward Closure.

##### **Phase Overview** The Follow-Up phase occurs after the active execution of the intervention plan.

##### During this phase, the team member reassesses the case’s current adherence-related situation after the home visit intervention and the targeted adherence coordination tasks have already been performed. The purpose of this phase is to confirm whether the operational gains achieved during the Intervention phase are being sustained, whether the case remains on track to maintain at least 80% PDC by the end of the calendar year, and whether any new or unresolved barriers require renewed intervention.

##### The Follow-Up phase functions as a reassessment and reinforcement phase. Unlike the Intervention phase, which focuses on active execution, the Follow-Up phase focuses on verifying continuity, reinforcing adherence behavior, monitoring the durability of the prior intervention, and identifying early signs of regression before the case deteriorates into a higher-risk state.

##### The Follow-Up phase consists of two structured stages:

##### **Stage 1 – Post-Intervention Reassessment,** and **Stage 2 – Ongoing Reinforcement** 

##### The first stage focuses on confirming whether the actions performed during the Intervention phase were completed and whether they produced the expected operational result. The second stage focuses on reinforcing adherence behavior, monitoring stability over time, and deciding whether the case should remain stable, return to Intervention, or proceed toward Closure.

##### Cases may cycle between Follow-Up and Intervention more than once during the calendar year. If follow-up findings indicate that the member has become unstable again, the case may return to Intervention. If the case remains stable and no further active work is currently indicated, it may proceed toward Closure when appropriate.

##### **Follow-Up Phase Entry Criteria**

##### The Follow-Up phase begins once the active intervention cycle has been completed and the case no longer requires immediate execution-phase actions, but still requires reassessment, reinforcement, or confirmation of sustained adherence performance.

##### To enter the Follow-Up phase, all of the following conditions must be met:

##### A)-The Intervention phase has been completed to the point that no immediate same-day or near-term corrective action remains pending.

##### B)-The member has received at least one active intervention, whether through Home Visit intervention, Phone Assessment, or both.

##### C)-The case has reached a sufficiently stable point to allow reassessment rather than immediate execution.

##### D)-A defined follow-up purpose exists, such as verifying medication pickup, confirming prescription renewal, reassessing adherence behavior, reviewing blood pressure self-monitoring, or checking whether previous barriers remain resolved.

##### E)-A follow-up timeline or trigger has been defined.

#####  **Follow-Up Phase Tasks**

#### Stage 1 – Post-Intervention Reassessment

#### **1-Reassessment of current case status**

##### The team member reassesses the member’s current adherence-related status following the Intervention phase.

##### This reassessment is intended to determine whether the member remains on an acceptable adherence trajectory, whether the prior intervention has had the intended operational effect, and whether the Reference Measure continues to reflect a stable condition.

##### This task may include, as applicable:

##### A)-Review of current refill progression.

##### B)-Confirmation of whether the medication has remained dispensed and available to the member.

##### C)-Confirmation of whether prescriptions requested during Intervention were actually received and remained usable.

##### D)-Review of whether the member appears to remain on track to maintain at least 80% PDC by the end of the calendar year.

##### E)-Confirmation of whether the most recent intervention outcome remains operationally valid.

#### 2-Verification of prior intervention completion

##### The team member verifies whether the actions initiated during the Intervention phase were fully completed and remain effective.

##### Examples include:

##### A)-Whether the member actually picked up or received the medication.

##### B)-Whether the prescription renewal that was promised was actually processed and remained available.

##### C)-Whether the member began using the pillbox appropriately.

##### D)-Whether the member began blood pressure self-monitoring as instructed.

##### E)-Whether educational materials and the medication list were retained and appear to remain useful.

##### F)-Whether a previously identified barrier appears reduced, unchanged, or worsened.

##### This task ensures that intervention success is not assumed merely because a planned action was initiated.

#### **3-Reassessment of barriers**

##### The team member reassesses both previously identified and newly emerging barriers that may be affecting adherence.

##### This includes, as applicable:

##### A)-Explicit barriers previously documented during Assessment.

##### B)-Implicit barriers identified during the home visit intervention.

##### C)-Any new operational barriers identified during follow-up contact.

##### D)-Any new social, behavioral, communication, or organizational factors interfering with adherence.

##### During this task, the team member determines whether the barriers remain resolved, partially resolved, unresolved, or newly emerged.

#### 

#### 

#### 

#### **4-Review of self-monitoring and outcome-related information**

##### When applicable, the team member reviews any information generated during the intervention period that may help the program understand whether adherence improvement is being accompanied by meaningful health-related change.

##### This may include:

##### A)-Review of the member’s reported blood pressure self-monitoring activity.

##### B)-Confirmation of whether blood pressure readings were taken twice daily as instructed for the requested period.

##### C)-Discussion of whether relevant lab test results were obtained or remain pending.

##### D)-Review of any other outcome-related information gathered within program scope.

##### This task supports the program’s broader goal of linking adherence-focused intervention with measurable health indicators over the life cycle of the case. 

#### Stage 2 – Ongoing Reinforcement and Stability Monitoring

#### **5-Reinforcement of adherence education**

##### During Follow-Up, the team member reinforces the key educational points delivered during the Intervention phase.

##### This includes, as applicable:

##### A)-Reinforcing the purpose of the monitored medications.

##### B)-Reinforcing the importance of timely refill, pickup, renewal, and daily medication-taking behavior.

##### C)-Reinforcing any medication-organization strategy introduced during the home visit, including use of the pillbox.

##### D)-Reinforcing blood pressure self-monitoring instructions when applicable.

##### E)-Reinforcing understanding through brief teach-back when appropriate.

##### The purpose of this task is not to repeat the full educational intervention, but to preserve and strengthen the member’s understanding and follow-through.

#### 

#### **6-Ongoing stability**

##### The team member monitors whether the case remains stable over time or shows early signs of regression.

##### This includes, as applicable:

##### A)-Review of refill continuity.

##### B)-Review of whether the member continues to have medication access.

##### C)-Review of whether previously resolved barriers remain controlled.

##### D)-Identification of any new signs of risk that may threaten future adherence performance.

##### E)-Confirmation that the member remains on an acceptable trajectory toward maintaining at least 80% PDC by the end of the calendar year.

##### This task is intended to identify instability early enough to intervene before the case deteriorates into a higher-risk state.

#### **7-Determination of whether the case remains stable or requires renewed intervention**

##### Following reassessment and stability monitoring, the team member determines whether the case should remain in Follow-Up, return to Intervention, or proceed toward Closure.

##### The case may:

##### A)-Remain in Follow-Up if the member is stable but still benefits from periodic reassessment.

##### B)-Return to Phase 4 Intervention if new findings indicate renewed adherence risk, operational instability, or unresolved barriers requiring active execution.

##### C)-Proceed toward Closure if the member has completed the active cycle and no further active work is currently indicated.

##### This task is essential because Follow-Up is not a passive holding phase. It is an active decision point that determines whether the prior intervention has truly stabilized the case.  **Follow-Up Phase Documentation**

##### All Follow-Up phase activity must be documented in the Internal Tracking on the columns named Assessment Results and Interventions 2 through 4 and, when applicable, on the Monthly Form. Documentation for this phase must include, as applicable:

##### A)-Date and time of each follow-up action.

##### B)-Follow-Up stage: \-Stage 1 Post-Intervention Reassessment. \-Stage 2 Ongoing Reinforcement   C)-Type of follow-up action performed: \-Member follow-up. \-Pharmacy follow-up. \-Prescriber follow-up. \-Education reinforcement. \-Barrier reassessment. \-Blood pressure monitoring review. \-Outcome-related information review.

##### D)-Name and role of the person reached, when contact is successful.

##### E)-Medication and measure relevant to the follow-up activity, including whether the action relates to the Reference Medication or Reference Measure.

##### F)-Outcome of each follow-up action.

##### G)-Whether prior intervention tasks were confirmed as completed.

##### H)-Whether barriers are resolved, partially resolved, unresolved, or newly identified.

##### I)-Whether the case remains stable, has worsened, or has improved.

##### J)-Any updated operational classification if the case status has changed.

##### K)-The resulting next action and next scheduled follow-up date, if applicable.

##### L)-Transition back to Intervention or forward toward Closure, when appropriate, and the reason for that transition.

### **Follow-Up Phase Exit Criteria**

##### The Follow-Up phase is considered complete when one of the following conditions has been met:

##### A)-The post-intervention reassessment has been completed and the case has entered a stable monitoring pattern with no immediate need for renewed intervention.

##### B)-The case remains stable and no further active reassessment is currently indicated, making it appropriate to proceed toward Closure.

##### C)-The case has demonstrated renewed instability, unresolved barriers, or new adherence risk requiring return to Phase 4 Intervention.

##### D)-The case has reached a new operational condition that justifies closure or another supervisor-directed disposition.

##### E)-The planned follow-up purpose has been completed and the next case decision has been clearly documented.

##### A case must not remain indefinitely in Follow-Up without a defined reason, purpose, or next decision point.

### **Follow-Up Phase Timeline**

##### The first Follow-Up action should occur according to the timeline defined at the end of the Intervention phase and based on the urgency and operational purpose of the follow-up.

##### Stage 1 – Post-Intervention Reassessment should occur first and within the timeframe required to confirm whether the prior intervention produced the intended operational result.

##### Stage 2 – Ongoing Reinforcement and Stability Monitoring should continue only as long as a defined follow-up purpose remains active and the case continues to benefit from reassessment or reinforcement.

##### Cases with recent high-risk status, unresolved barriers, or recent prescription-related instability should receive earlier follow-up.

##### Cases that have stabilized after intervention may receive follow-up at a less intensive interval, provided the reason for the timing is clear and documented.

##### All scheduled follow-up actions, including member calls, pharmacy checks, prescriber confirmations, and review of self-monitoring information, must be completed within the timeline committed during the prior phase. Any delay, missed contact, or failed reassessment attempt must be documented and followed by a defined next action.

##### If operational capacity is exceeded, follow-up work should be prioritized according to the current urgency of the Reference Measure and the likelihood that delayed reassessment could allow the case to regress into a higher-risk state.

##### **Phase 6 \- Closure**

##### **Objective**

##### Formally conclude the active lifecycle of the case once the member has either completed the intervention and follow-up process, become ineligible for continued participation, refused services, or reached a point at which no further active work is currently necessary. The purpose of this phase is to ensure that the final case disposition is accurately classified, the reason for closure is clearly documented, the final operational status is recorded, and the case is properly prepared for transition into Monitoring when applicable.

### **Phase Overview**

##### The Closure phase is the formal endpoint of the active portion of the workflow.

##### During this phase, the team member confirms that active work on the case should end, records the final operational disposition of the case, and completes all required closing documentation. Closure does not necessarily mean that the member is no longer relevant to the program. Rather, it means that the case no longer requires active outreach, assessment, intervention, or follow-up at that time. Upon completion of the Closure phase, all cases transition into the Monitoring phase. Monitoring is the inactive phase of the workflow and applies to all cases regardless of whether they completed the active cycle, refused services, were unreachable, or were found ineligible for enrollment.

##### The Closure phase functions as the control gate between active case management and inactive case observation. It ensures that cases do not remain active without a defined operational purpose and that all cases exiting the active workflow are closed in a standardized, auditable, and reportable manner.

##### A case may enter Closure from multiple prior phases. Some cases close early, such as those found to be ineligible during Intake, those refusing services during Contact, or those remaining unreachable after the maximum number of contact attempts. Other cases reach Closure only after completion of Intervention and Follow-Up, when no further active work is currently necessary. For this reason, the Closure phase must support both early closure and end-of-cycle closure.

##### When applicable, Closure also prepares the case for transition into the Monitoring phase, during which inactive cases remain under refill-event observation for potential re-enrollment.  **Closure Phase Entry Criteria**

##### The Closure phase begins once a case has met at least one condition that justifies discontinuation of active workflow progression.

##### A case may enter the Closure phase from any prior active phase when one or more of the following conditions has been met:

##### A)-The case has been found operationally ineligible for continued participation in the program.

##### B)-The member or authorized representative has explicitly refused services.

##### C)-The member has remained unreachable after the maximum allowed outreach attempts.

##### D)-The member has deferred enrollment or intervention beyond the program-defined allowable timeframe.

##### E)-The member is confirmed to have expired.

##### F)-The member is confirmed to be hospitalized, institutionalized, or otherwise unavailable for participation in a manner that makes continued active work operationally inappropriate.

##### G)-The Intervention and Follow-Up phases have been completed and no further active work is currently necessary.

##### H)-The direct supervisor has instructed that the case be closed.

##### **Closure Phase Tasks**

##### **1-Confirmation of closure appropriateness**

##### Before closing the case, the team member confirms that closure is operationally appropriate and supported by documented findings.

##### This task ensures that closure is not used prematurely when the case still has a defined and reasonably active next step. The triggering condition must be verified based on the phase in which the case currently sits.

##### Examples include:

##### A)-Confirmation that five unsuccessful contact attempts were completed when closure is due to inability to reach.

##### B)-Confirmation that all available contact numbers were invalid when closure is due to wrong number or disconnected lines.

##### C)-Confirmation that the member or authorized representative explicitly declined services when closure is due to refusal.

##### D)-Confirmation that the member is outside the service area, presents a disqualifying language barrier, has an ineligible health plan status, or has another documented eligibility barrier when closure is due to ineligibility.

##### E)-Confirmation that the member has expired, is hospitalized, institutionalized, or unavailable for an extended period when closure is due to circumstantial ineligibility.

##### F)-Confirmation that Intervention and Follow-Up have been completed and that no further active action is currently necessary when closure follows successful completion of the active workflow.

##### Closure must always be supported by documented operational findings, not by assumption. 

##### **2-Assignment of closure reason**

##### Once closure is confirmed as appropriate, the case is assigned a standardized closure reason.

##### The closure reason must reflect the primary operational reason the case is being discontinued from active work. Only one primary closure reason should be assigned for reporting purposes, even if multiple contributing factors are present.

##### Recommended closure reasons include:

##### A)-Interventions Completed.

##### B)-Refused Services. (With Clarification if refusal was before or after enrollment)

##### C)-Unable to Reach. (With clarification of either 5 attempts completed, Wrong Phone Number or Phone Disconnected.)

##### D)-Not Eligible Before Enrollment. (With clarification of Outside of Service Area, Hospitalized, Expired, Language Barrier, Disenrolled from Health Plan.)  E)-Not Eligible Before Enrollment. (With clarification of Outside of Service Area, Hospitalized, Expired, Language Barrier, Disenrolled from Health Plan.)

#### **3-Final case status review**

##### Before the case is formally closed, the team member performs a final review to ensure that the record accurately reflects the most recent known status of the case.

##### This review includes, as applicable:

##### A)-Most recent phase completed.

##### B)-Most recent enrollment status.

##### C)-Whether contact with the member or representative was ever achieved.

##### D)-Whether a home visit was completed.

##### E)-Whether Intervention was partially completed, fully completed, or not completed.

##### F)-Whether Follow-Up was completed.

##### G)-Most recent Reference Measure and Case Matrix position, when relevant.

##### H)-Most recent Prescription Status and Adherence Risk Status, when relevant.

##### I)-Whether explicit and/or implicit barriers were identified.

##### J)-Whether any meaningful intervention outcomes were achieved before closure.

##### K)-Whether the case remains appropriate for future re-enrollment if risk reappears during Monitoring.

##### This task ensures that Closure preserves the operational history of the case and that the final record remains useful for reporting, auditing, and future workflow decisions.

#### **5-Transition to Monitoring Phase**

##### All cases remain relevant for future refill-risk observation, the closed cases are transitioned into the Monitoring phase. All closures require future monitoring regardless of previous enrollment status. 

##### **Closure Phase Documentation**

##### All Closure phase activity must be documented in the Internal Tracking and the Monthly Form.

##### Documentation for this phase must include, as applicable:

##### A)-Date of closure.

##### B)-Primary closure reason.

##### C)-Detailed reason for closure.

##### D)-Phase from which the case entered Closure.

##### E)-Enrollment status at the time of closure.

##### F)-Whether contact with the member or representative was ever achieved.

##### G)-Whether a home visit was completed.

##### H)-Whether Intervention was partially completed, fully completed, or not completed.

##### I)-Whether Follow-Up was completed.

##### J)-Any most recent operational classification relevant to the case, including Reference Measure, Prescription Status, Adherence Risk Status, and Case Matrix position when applicable

##### K)-Any final supervisor instruction relevant to the closure.

##### L)-Any final narrative note necessary to clarify the case disposition.

##### When closure occurs early in the workflow, the documentation should clearly indicate that the case did not progress through all active phases. When closure occurs after completion of Intervention and Follow-Up, the documentation should clearly indicate that active work ended because no further active action was currently necessary. This information should be placed on the Internal Tracking document, specifically on the column named Assessment Results.

### **Closure Phase Exit Criteria**

##### The Closure phase is considered complete when all of the following conditions have been met:

##### A)-Closure appropriateness has been confirmed.

##### B)-A primary closure reason has been assigned.

##### C)-The final case status review has been completed.

##### D)-All required final documentation has been entered into the Internal Tracking and Monthly Form.

##### E)-The case has been formally removed from active workflow status.

##### Upon completion of these criteria, the case may proceed to the Monitoring phase.  **Closure Phase Timeline**

##### The Closure phase should be completed on the same business day that the closure decision is made whenever operationally possible.

##### When same-day completion is not possible, closure documentation should be completed within one business day of the closure-triggering event or decision.

##### Cases entering Closure due to refusal, inability to reach, invalid contact information, confirmed ineligibility, or supervisor instruction should be dispositioned promptly so they do not remain incorrectly classified as active.

##### Cases closing after successful Completion of Interventions should also be closed promptly once it has been determined that no further active work is currently necessary.

##### If operational capacity is exceeded, closure processing should still be prioritized for cases that are no longer appropriate for active work, in order to preserve tracker accuracy, workload integrity, and reporting reliability.

##### **Phase 7 \- Monitoring**

### **Objective**

##### Monitor refill-related patterns for all closed cases in order to identify current or emerging nonadherence risk, preserve visibility over the full referred population through the end of the case lifecycle, and flag previously enrolled members for potential re-enrollment when refill behavior indicates renewed need for active intervention.

### **Phase Overview**

##### The Monitoring phase is the inactive phase of the workflow and is experienced by all cases after Closure.

##### During this phase, the case no longer receives active outreach, assessment, intervention, or follow-up. Instead, the team monitors refill-related events and adherence-related signals to determine whether the case remains stable or whether renewed risk is emerging. The purpose of this phase is to preserve operational visibility over all referred members, including those who completed the active cycle, refused services, were unreachable, or were otherwise closed before enrollment.

##### The Monitoring phase functions as the surveillance and reactivation gate of the workflow. It ensures that closed cases do not disappear from program awareness and that refill-pattern deterioration can be identified in time to support future action when appropriate.

##### Monitoring applies to all closed cases regardless of closure reason or prior enrollment status. However, the possibility of **re-enrollment** applies specifically to members who were previously enrolled and for whom new refill-related risk suggests renewed need for active work. This distinction is operationally important: all cases are monitored, but only selected cases are flagged for possible return to active phases of the workflow.

##### This approach is aligned with the broader program logic, in which refill timing, runout timing, prior failures, and continuously updated adherence signals are central to prioritization and intervention planning. 

### **Monitoring Phase Entry Criteria**

##### The Monitoring phase begins immediately upon completion of the Closure phase.

##### A case enters the Monitoring phase when all of the following conditions have been met:

##### A)-The case has completed the Closure phase.

##### B)-The case has been removed from active workflow status.

##### C)-The final closure reason and case disposition have been documented.

##### D)-The case has been formally transitioned into inactive monitoring status.

##### Monitoring applies to all closed cases, including but not limited to:

##### A)-Cases that completed the active cycle.

##### B)-Cases that refused services.

##### C)-Cases that were unreachable.

##### D)-Cases that were closed due to ineligibility or circumstantial barriers.

##### E)-Cases that were previously enrolled and later stabilized.

### **Monitoring Phase Tasks**

#### **1-Refill-pattern surveillance**

##### The team member reviews refill-related information for closed cases on a weekly basis in order to detect signals of current or future nonadherence risk.

##### This surveillance icludes, as applicable:

##### A)-Review of updated refill timing.

##### B)-Review of next fill due dates.

##### C)-Review of runout timing or projected medication exhaustion.

##### D)-Review of refills remaining.

##### E)-Review of recent claims activity.

##### F)-Review of reversed claims.

##### The purpose of this task is to identify whether the case remains operationally quiet or whether refill behavior suggests renewed instability.   **2-Identification of renewed adherence risk**

##### Using the refill-pattern information available during Monitoring, the team member determines whether the closed case shows signs of renewed adherence risk.

##### Examples of renewed risk may include:

##### A)-Evidence that a monitored medication is approaching or has passed its expected refill date.

##### B)-Evidence of declining refill continuity.

##### C)-Evidence that prior stability may no longer be sustained.

##### D)-Evidence that the member may again be trending toward failure to maintain adherence performance at or above 80% PDC.

##### This task does not reopen the case by itself. Its purpose is to classify whether the monitored case remains stable or should be flagged for additional review.

#### **3-Flagging of previously enrolled members for potential re-enrollment**

##### The purpose of flagging is not to bypass eligibility rules or lifecycle discipline. Rather, it is to identify candidates for formal reassessment and possible creation of a new active episode if future rules and eligibility conditions support it. Flagged cases should be brought to the attention of the supervisor which will communicate the findings to our partner health plan. 

#### **4-Maintenance of inactive case visibility**

##### The team member ensures that the case remains visible in the inactive population throughout the monitoring period and that its current monitoring status is accurately reflected in the program’s tracking systems.

##### This task supports:

##### A)-Population-level visibility.

##### B)-Future risk review.

##### C)-Operational reporting.

### **Monitoring Phase Documentation**

##### During the Monitoring phase documentation should be minimal and only sufficient to explain why a case is being flagged for potential re-enrollment. 

### **Monitoring Phase Exit Criteria**

##### The Monitoring phase continues until the end of the case lifecycle unless one of the following occurs:

##### A)-The monitoring period reaches its program-defined endpoint on December 31, 2026\.

##### C)-A new active episode is created under the appropriate eligibility and lifecycle rules.

##### For the purposes of this workflow, Monitoring is the final phase of the current case lifecycle. A case may remain in Monitoring through the end of the calendar year.  **Monitoring Phase Timeline**

##### Monitoring begins immediately upon completion of Closure.

##### Monitoring reviews should occur on a weekly basis consistent with the cadence of available refill-related data sources and the operational needs of the program. Because the program depends heavily on refill timing, updated claims information, and pharmacy-ground-truth data, monitoring cadence should be frequent enough to detect renewed risk before a case becomes unsalvageable.
